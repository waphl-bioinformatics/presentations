Analytical Specificity / Interference

Analytical specificity refers to the ability of a test or procedure to correctly identify or quantify an entity in the presence of interfering or cross-reactive substances that might be expected to be present.

Required Analysis Metrics

Clearly define the measurements to determine accuracy and reproducibility.

Accuracy

Are your test results correct? This can be determined by testing previously annotated genomes, side-by-side testing with a reference laboratory, proficiency testing materials that have established values, and previously tested patient specimens with established values. Total accuracy should be at least 90% relative to the reference method.

Accuracy (Agreement) = 100 × (No. of correct results / Total no. of results)

Reproducibility

Can you obtain the same result time after time? Confirm this parameter by repeatedly testing the same samples on different days, using different computers to interface with the bioinformatic pipeline, and obtaining the same or comparable results (reproducible) regardless of which member of the laboratory’s testing personnel performs the test (operator variance).

For fully automated test systems that are not operator dependent, operator variance should not affect the test’s precision and may not need to be evaluated by more than one person. Acceptable precision is considered calculated reproducibility ≥ 95%.

Reproducibility = 100 × (No. of repeated results in agreement / Total no. of results)

Analytical Sensitivity

Analytical sensitivity measures the number of quantifiable samples at specified concentration levels near the expected detection limit. This only applies to qualitative tests.

Sensitivity = [TP / (TP + FN)] × 100

Specificity = [TN / (TN + FP)] × 100

Lower Limit of Detection (LOD)

LOD may be influenced by genome size and is required for each variant and microbial target type. For antiviral drug resistance, LOD establishment must consider the viral load and variant allele fraction.
